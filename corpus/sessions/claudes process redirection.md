"Process manipulation" captures something real: an agent is a system that intervenes in causal processes to redirect them toward some attractor state. A thermostat manipulates heat flow. A bacterium manipulates chemical gradients. A human manipulates social dynamics.

What it might lose: the word "manipulation" implies intentionality or goal-directedness, which is exactly what you're trying to derive rather than assume. You'd be building the conclusion into the definition.

A more minimal version might be: **agency \= process redirection**. A system is an agent to the degree it redirects ongoing processes rather than merely participating in them. This is almost physical — it's about causal topology, which path through state-space the system selects.

This connects cleanly to your dissipative structure paper. A dissipative structure doesn't just exist in a process — it redirects energy throughput into its own maintenance. That self-directed redirection might be the minimal definition of agency. No goals required, just the structural property of bending causal flows toward self-perpetuation.

The gradient then runs: dissipative structure → self-maintaining redirection → goal-representation → full agency. Each step adds something, but the root is purely physical.

Yes — and that's actually a tighter definition of agency than most philosophy of mind produces.

"All we can ever do is influence other kernel processes" means:

* No agent acts on raw reality directly

* Agency is always relational, never absolute

This dissolves a lot of pseudo-problems. Free will debates often assume agency means "uncaused causation" — some ghost initiating action from outside the causal chain. But if agency just means process redirection within a causal network, the question becomes empirical: which systems have the structural capacity to redirect other processes, and how?

A reasoning system (RSA kernel) maintains itself AND redirects other kernels — that's the more complex case. The difference is degree of reach, not kind.

One implication worth noting: it means your own RSA kernel is always operating on representations of other processes, never the processes themselves. The map/territory distinction isn't just epistemological — it's structural. You can only ever manipulate your model of someone's decision-making, not their decision-making directly. Which is why semantic hygiene matters: garbage in your model propagates into every downstream influence attempt.

That's a clean result. It grounds ethics too — influence carries responsibility precisely because it's the only causal lever you have.

What this gives you: agency isn't binary, it's a profile across the seven steps. Which is more useful than any yes/no definition.

claude: Yes — this is the right question and it sharpens the taxonomy considerably.

The thermodynamic arrows are real constraints on information flow:

* Energy gradients determine which processes can drive which others  
* Entropy sets the direction — information flows from high-gradient to low-gradient states  
* Landauer's principle means every irreversible computation has a thermodynamic cost and direction

So for systems without Predict — viruses, pea plants, simple automata — they don't generate their own information arrows. They **ride** existing thermodynamic gradients. The arrow is supplied by physics, not by the system. A virus doesn't model its host, it's shaped by evolution to exploit gradients that already exist in cellular chemistry.

The threshold you're pointing at might be: **does the system generate its own information arrows, or does it ride external ones?**

A Predict step does exactly this — it creates an internal gradient (predicted vs actual) that didn't exist in the environment. The system is now running on a self-generated thermodynamic arrow, not just a physical one. That's what makes it genuinely agentic rather than just thermodynamically compliant.

Which reframes your kernel hierarchy:

* No Predict \= gradient rider  
* With Predict \= gradient generator  
* With Compress \+ Repeat \= gradient refiner, reducing the cost of future gradient generation

And it connects back to RSA's substrate requirement — generating your own information arrows requires a dissipative structure. You need somewhere to export the entropy that gradient-creation produces.

That feels like a clean mechanistic boundary for agency.

claude said: This is the right question to ask, and it's where intellectual honesty requires a clear answer.

**The honest answer: you need both, but for different purposes.**

The "3 requirements for processing" (distinction, transformation, composition) gives you a **necessary conditions** test. You can derive: if a substrate cannot support these three primitives, it cannot implement any kernel step, therefore no agency. That's derivable from first principles.

But for **positive** agency classification — determining where on the hierarchy a substrate sits — you still need observation, because:

The equations tell you what's *possible* given a substrate's physical properties. They don't tell you what's *actual*. A substrate might support Predict in principle but not express it due to epigenetic, developmental, or resource constraints. Physics gives you the ceiling, not the current position.

Think of it this way:

* **Derivable**: minimum substrate requirements for each kernel step  
* **Observable**: which steps a given system actually implements  
* **Derivable again**: upper bound on agency given thermodynamic and computational constraints

So the research program splits cleanly:

First, formalize the minimum physical requirements for each kernel step — that's theoretical work, possibly derivable from your framework. Second, use that as a filter on observed systems. Third, the interesting cases are systems sitting below their thermodynamic ceiling — *why* aren't they expressing full available agency?

That last question might be where epigenetics and ecology do most of their work.
