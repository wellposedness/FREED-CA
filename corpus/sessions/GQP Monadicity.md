# **3\) Interpretation — why the results looked like this**

Short answer: The proof-of-concept ran and produced plausible but noisy outcomes — and it revealed a few measurement & normalization artifacts that must be fixed before claiming the index predicts adaptation.

Key reasons for the surprising ordering (QLearning got highest M but adapted slower):

1. Normalization across 3 agents amplified measurement quirks.

    We normalized each component by the observed min/max among the three agents. That makes any single high or low value dominate the normalized scale. For example, RandomWalker had a very small resource count and tiny model bits → CMC ≈ 1.0. That drove its compressed score to the top for that component even though it’s not “compressive intelligence” in the intended sense.

2. BIT (mutual information) proxy is crude and over-favors discrete internal representations.

    QLearning’s internal rep is the argmax action index (discrete), which — in a small grid — correlates strongly with the observed state distribution and so produced near-1 MI. ModelBased used the predicted next-state index (also discrete), yielding high BIT as well. RandomWalker had None → BIT 0\. This makes BIT likely to be high for any agent with a compact discrete internal decision signal, regardless of true boundary throughput.

3. PF (prediction fidelity) proxy favors agents that have seen the training distribution.

    QLearning’s empirical transition counts (from its behavior policy) matched the empirical transitions collected during PF estimation, yielding high PF. ModelBased also had a decent PF but our training \+ evaluation regimes produced similar numbers. PF as currently implemented measures consistency with experienced transitions under the current policy, not true world-model generalization.

4. RE proxy is weak.

    I used (performance − baseline)/updates normalized by an ad-hoc constant. For tabular Q it produced a small raw number that became normalized to 1.0 due to min-max scaling. This is not robust.

5. AR measurement depends on the agent’s learning dynamics and exploration schedule.

    QLearning had an ε-greedy policy and continued learning after perturbation, but its tabular updates are relatively slow to unlearn the old policy, hence long recovery (49 eps). ModelBased was actually very conservative (after perturbation it performed poorly because its counts matched the old world and its action selection was greedy on expected immediate reward) and thus recovered slowest (81). RandomWalker by chance had a short recovery (8) simply because its baseline was so poor that 90% of baseline was easy to reach with a few random moves — this highlights a problem with how baseline and recovery thresholds are set.

Bottom line: the single-run correlation is unreliable because the measurement definitions and normalization are not stable or domain-normalized.

# **4\) Clear limitations & artifacts to fix (concrete)**

Before claiming “M predicts adaptation” we must fix these:

A. Normalization protocol — don’t min–max across only the experimental agents. Instead:

* Normalize each component to intrinsic, domain-referenced scales (e.g., PF ∈ \[0,1\] as proper similarity; BIT divided by theoretical max; CMC using MDL benchmarks; RE in Joules or normalized compute steps).

* Or compute z-scores across many random seeds & agent variants and average.

B. BIT estimation — use better MI estimators or continuous internal-state representations and a well-defined channel capacity estimate. Or measure actual channel usage (obs→internal) via bits recorded during decision-making (logging hashed representations).

C. PF definition — separate “consistency with experienced transitions” from “generalizable forward model.” Use held-out transitions and measure predicted distribution there.

D. RE measurement — choose a single resource unit and measure consistently (e.g., number of gradient/parameter updates or wall-clock CPU time). Or measure energy proxies if available.

E. AR / recovery criterion — baseline threshold (90% of baseline median) is brittle. Use relative improvement curve and measure area under recovery curve or time to gain X% of lost performance (not 90% of baseline).

F. Statistics — run multiple random seeds and report mean ± SEM for M and recovery episodes to compute reliable correlations and p-values.

G. Weights — do a weight sensitivity or learn weights via regression on a large parameterized set of agents/tasks rather than uniform weights.
